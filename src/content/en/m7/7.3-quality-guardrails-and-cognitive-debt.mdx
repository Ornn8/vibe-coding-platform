<Callout title="Method Focus" tone="warning">
The more agents you add and the faster they generate, the more guardrails matter. Scale amplifies defects as easily as it amplifies throughput.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Why scale magnifies the problem

If one agent produces a few changes a day, defects may stay hidden for a while. If a system produces many changes continuously, even a low error rate quickly becomes a steady stream of bad output.

### Scenario

The curriculum's example is meant to make the math visible: a tiny error rate becomes a large number of defects once the workflow is producing at scale. Cognitive debt grows in the same way. Small mistakes pile up until the review cost becomes the real problem.

## Step 2: Let automation catch mechanical failures

Linting, tests, security scanning, and type checks belong in the machine layer. They do not replace thought, but they block large classes of repeatable failures before human review starts.

### Scenario

The point of guardrails is to move easy mistakes out of the human review queue:
- lint catches style and syntax problems
- tests catch behavior regressions
- security scans catch avoidable risks

That frees human review for the harder calls.

## Step 3: Decide what humans must still review

Architecture tradeoffs, business logic, permission boundaries, user impact, and long-term maintainability often still require human judgment. Good review is not about reading everything manually. It is about reserving human attention for the things machines still judge poorly.

### Scenario

The curriculum's review checklist splits work into three buckets:
- must be machine-checked
- must be human-reviewed
- must never auto-merge without a person

That split keeps the team from over-trusting automation.

## Summary
- Scale increases risk as well as output.
- Automation should handle mechanical checks first.
- Human review should be reserved for judgment calls and risk boundaries.
