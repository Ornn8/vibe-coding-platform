<Callout type="methodology" title="Methodology Focus">
Projects rarely fail because the tool suddenly becomes bad. They fail because ambiguity is handed to AI as if it were a plan.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Project failure is usually gradual

Here is a common six-round failure pattern for a vocabulary-learning app.

| Round | What you asked | What AI produced | How it felt at the time | The real problem |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Build me a vocabulary app | Login, home, word list, rankings, and more | It looked impressive | You never defined the user or core flow |
| 2 | Add a check-in feature | Streaks and points appeared | It felt like progress | Scope started expanding |
| 3 | Make the UI nicer | Colors and cards changed | It looked more polished | The core flow was still unclear |
| 4 | Add favorites and wrong-answer review | IA expanded again | It felt feature-rich | The MVP disappeared |
| 5 | Why is it getting messy | AI tried to reorganize everything | The project became fragmented | Earlier rounds had no boundary |
| 6 | Forget it, let's restart | You threw it away | It felt like a reset | Several rounds were wasted |

<Callout type="scenario" title="Scenario: The earliest warning sign">
Situation: AI gives you a lot of screens and features in round one, and that feels exciting.

Action: Pause and ask which of those things you actually requested.

Why: The earliest danger is often not "too little output," but "too much output with no target."
</Callout>

The first round often does not feel like failure. The damage usually becomes visible in rounds three to six.

## Step 2: The real root cause usually lives in the requirement stage

If you start with "build me a blog" or "build me a vocabulary app," AI will make many hidden decisions for you: who the user is, what the critical flow should be, what the first version includes, and what should be excluded.

| Requirement wording | What AI will tend to fill in by default | Main risk |
|---|---|---|
| Build a blog | Login, comments, tags, categories, admin | The project becomes large and diffuse |
| Build a first blog version for independent writers with only a home page, article page, and basic nav | The structure stays tighter | The core assumption is easier to validate |

<Callout type="warning" title="Before / After: Fuzzy start vs bounded start">
Wrong: Build me a blog.

Right: Build a first blog version for independent writers with only a home page, article page, and basic navigation. Do not include comments, login, or admin.

Difference: The wrong version tells AI to expand. The right version tells AI where to stop.
</Callout>

The point of M2 is not to make prompts longer. It is to make requirements more executable.

## Step 3: How to stop a crash before round one

For any real project idea, write at least three lines before you ask AI to build:

- Who the product is for
- What the first version must validate
- What this round explicitly does not include

<Callout type="scenario" title="Scenario: Blocking a crash before it starts">
Situation: You are about to start a new project such as a reading-notes tool.

Action: Write the user, core validation goal, and explicit exclusions before the first AI request.

Why: That determines whether AI converges or expands.
</Callout>

| Pre-start check | What happens if it is missing | What happens if it is clear |
|---|---|---|
| Target user | AI designs for "everyone" | The scope narrows |
| Validation goal | AI tries to build everything | The first version becomes focused |
| Explicit exclusions | AI keeps inventing more features | Rework drops sharply |

## Unit Summary

- Project failure is usually cumulative, not sudden.
- AI amplifies fuzzy requirements instead of fixing them.
- The best time to stop a crash is before round one.

**Methodology Review**: Planning-driven work is not slower. It removes the most expensive ambiguity early.
