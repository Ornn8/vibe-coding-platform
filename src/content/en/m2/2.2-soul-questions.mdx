<Callout type="methodology" title="Methodology Focus">
The soul questions are the minimum planning layer before any AI build: who is using this, what hurts, and why should they choose this solution.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Who Is the User?

Before you ask AI to build anything, define a real person or a clearly scoped group. "Everyone" is not a user. A good user description includes context, habits, and the situation in which the product is used.

When the user is concrete, the rest of the project becomes easier. The page structure, tone, flow, and feature choices all become more grounded.

Consider the difference:

| Weak user description | Strong user description |
|-----------------------|-------------------------|
| People who want to learn | A college student who wants to review vocab on the bus between classes |
| Anyone who needs notes | A freelance designer who tracks client ideas on mobile during meetings |

## Step 2: What Pain Are You Solving?

Good products solve a specific pain, not an abstract desire. The pain can be obvious, like "I waste too much time," or subtle, like "I do not know what to do next." If you cannot name the pain, AI will usually fill the gap with guesses.

The goal is not to write a marketing slogan. The goal is to describe a problem precise enough that implementation decisions can be made later.

You can test the quality of the pain statement by asking whether the current workaround is obvious. If the user already has a decent way to solve the problem, your solution needs to beat that specific workaround.

## Step 3: Why This Solution

Once the user and pain are clear, explain why your idea should exist. This is the place to state the angle, the difference, or the reason this is better for the chosen user than the obvious alternatives.

This question matters because it helps you avoid building a generic clone. AI is very good at making generic things that look complete. Your job is to keep the project pointed at a real difference.

<Separator />

## Step 4: Combine the Three Answers

The final step is to put the user, pain, and solution together in a form that AI can actually use. That means the statement should read like a build brief, not like a slogan.

When the three parts fit, later planning becomes much easier because the project has a clear direction and a clear reason to exist.
