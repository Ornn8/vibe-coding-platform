<Callout type="methodology" title="Methodology Focus">
The soul questions are the lightest useful planning move in the requirement stage. They stop drift before AI starts building.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Question one, who is the user

Do not write "everyone," "young people," or "people who want to learn." A usable persona should include role, situation, and current behavior.

<Callout type="scenario" title="Scenario: What a useful persona looks like">
Weak: For people who want to learn English.

Better: For first-year college students who review vocabulary during a 30-minute commute.

Why: The second version tells you when the user opens the product and what the homepage must prioritize.
</Callout>

| Description style | How AI will read it | Resulting difference |
|---|---|---|
| People who want to learn English | General-purpose audience | The product becomes broad and generic |
| Students reviewing vocabulary during their commute | Clear usage context | The first version becomes more focused |

Specific users naturally create narrower scope.

## Step 2: Question two, where is the pain

Real pain is not "this would be nice." Real pain is a repeated cost in time, attention, or money. A strong test is to ask how the user is currently hacking around the problem.

<Callout type="scenario" title="Scenario: Real pain vs fake demand">
Fake demand: I want a cooler homepage.

Real pain: The user manually sorts expense screenshots and retypes them into a spreadsheet every day.

Why: The first is product imagination. The second is an actual cost the user already pays.
</Callout>

| Statement | More like what | Why |
|---|---|---|
| The homepage should feel cooler | Fake demand | No clear user cost is attached |
| The user repeatedly organizes screenshots and amounts by hand | Real pain | There is a measurable inefficient behavior |

If you cannot describe the user's current workaround, the problem is probably still too vague.

## Step 3: Question three, why this solution

"Why this solution" does not mean claiming you are better than every competitor. It means narrowing the product to one concrete advantage: faster, lighter, easier, or more suitable for a specific context.

<Callout type="warning" title="Before / After: Slogan vs usable differentiation">
Wrong: I want to build an app that is better than every competitor.

Right: I want to build a vocabulary tool that works for five-minute commute sessions.

Difference: The wrong version has no boundary. The right version does.
</Callout>

| Solution statement | Executable? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Better than everyone | No | No specific advantage is defined |
| Built for short commute sessions | Yes | The value and boundary are clear |

Once question three is clear, the project becomes a bounded solution rather than a vague idea.

## Unit Summary

- Specific users naturally shrink the scope.
- Real pain usually means the user already tolerates a clumsy workaround.
- Differentiation is about boundary, not slogans.
