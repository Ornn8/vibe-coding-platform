<Callout title="Method Focus" tone="warning">
The capstone is not "one more page." It is proof that you can connect every earlier method into one full loop of topic selection, planning, implementation, release, and retrospective.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Pick from five concrete directions, not from a vague blank page
The hardest part of a capstone is often not building it. It is choosing something real, bounded, and demoable.

| Direction | Complexity | Better fit for | What problem it solves |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI course learning dashboard | Low | Learners who just finished M4-M5 | Shows lessons, progress, bookmarks, and reminders |
| Submission review console | Medium | People who want CRUD plus permissions practice | Manages student submissions, status, and review notes |
| Content authoring and publishing workspace | Medium | People who want editing-flow and content-system practice | Manages drafts, publishing, tags, and revisions |
| Lightweight customer-support knowledge assistant | Medium-high | People who want search, knowledge workflows, and AI calls | Answers internal FAQs and organizes knowledge assets |
| Lightweight team collaboration tool | High | People aiming for a stronger full-stack portfolio piece | Handles tasks, permissions, notifications, and shared workflows |

<Callout title="Scenario: why the topic cannot be chosen only by coolness" tone="info">
Situation: you want to build a very ambitious AI SaaS because it sounds impressive. Action: first ask whether it can be completed, launched, and demoed within your actual time window. Why: the capstone is judged by loop completion, not by how large the idea sounds.
</Callout>

## Step 2: The capstone deliverable is an evidence chain, not only code
The full capstone package should include at least:

| Deliverable | Why it must be there |
|---|---|
| Repository | Shows what you actually built |
| Deployment link | Shows other people can access and demo it |
| PRD, plan, or feature docs | Shows you did not build by pure impulse |
| `CLAUDE.md`, Skills, and memory bank | Shows you can organize an AI collaboration system |
| Retrospective | Shows you can extract method from the project |

```text
Before: I thought the capstone meant turning in one finished page.
After: I understand that the capstone really submits result plus process plus methodology evidence.
```

## Step 3: The final retrospective decides whether you actually leveled up
A strong retrospective should answer:

| Review question | What you need to explain |
|---|---|
| Why the topic was right | Why this topic matched your current stage and time window |
| How the methodology was used | Where you used soul questions, PEV, Skills, and the memory bank |
| Where the most rework happened | Which step lost boundary control most easily |
| What changes next time | Which rules should become long-term reusable assets |

<Callout title="before / after: project finished vs project becomes the next starting point" tone="warning">
Wrong move: treat the capstone as a one-time showcase. Better move: use the retrospective to capture topic judgment, execution rhythm, rework lessons, and toolchain assets. One is just an ending. The other becomes the starting advantage for the next project.
</Callout>

## Summary
- A capstone topic should be real, finishable, and demoable.
- The true capstone deliverable is code, release output, and a process evidence chain.
- The retrospective is what turns this project into a stronger starting point for the next one.

## Methodology Review
Closed-loop delivery is fully demonstrated here: from topic choice to launch to retrospective, every stage leaves behind results that can be inspected, explained, and reused.
