<Callout type="methodology" title="Methodology Focus">
Before AI can do good work, it needs a role and a task. If those are vague, the output usually looks right while still being wrong.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Define the Role

The role tells AI what kind of behavior to adopt: planner, editor, reviewer, teacher, or builder. A good role is concrete enough to influence tone and decision-making, but not so dramatic that it becomes useless.

Role definition is one of the simplest ways to improve prompt quality.

Examples:

- Weak: "You are an expert."
- Strong: "You are a product reviewer who checks scope, usability, and clarity."
- Stronger: "You are a curriculum editor who rewrites lesson drafts for clarity without changing the lesson goal."

## Step 2: Define the Task

The task tells AI what it must actually accomplish. "Help me with this" is too weak. A strong task says what the output should do, where it will be used, and what success looks like.

When the task is precise, the model spends less time guessing and more time producing useful output.

## Step 3: Combine Role and Task

Role and task work best together. If the role is "product reviewer" but the task is "fix the CSS," the prompt is already confused. A strong prompt makes the role and task reinforce each other.

That is the first half of the four-element frame.

<Separator />

## Step 4: Make the Combination Clear Enough to Use

When role and task are both strong, the prompt becomes easier to review, easier to improve, and easier to reuse later. That is the point of this frame: the prompt should say who AI is being, what it is doing, and why that combination makes sense.

If you can read the prompt out loud and immediately understand the job, you are probably close.

For example, "You are a product reviewer. Review this onboarding flow and point out where users may get confused" is much clearer than "You are an expert. Help me improve this."

## Unit Summary

- Role tells AI how to behave.
- Task tells AI what to accomplish.
- Good prompts align both pieces instead of mixing them loosely.
