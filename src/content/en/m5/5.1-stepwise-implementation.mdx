<Callout title="Method Focus" tone="warning">
Closed-loop delivery is not about slowing AI down. It is about making every step verifiable, stoppable, and reversible.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Compare Three Real Step Sizes
Imagine you need to add "user login and registration" to a learning platform. Here are three common decompositions:

| Decomposition | What it sounds like | What is wrong with it |
|---|---|---|
| Too coarse | Build the whole auth system | The scope is too large, and the errors will blur together |
| Too fine | First add one input, then one button, then one line of validation | The management cost becomes absurdly high |
| Well sized | Form structure -> validation -> submit API -> signed-in state display | Each step has a visible completion signal |

```text
Before: ask AI to build login and registration in one shot.
After: build the form structure first, then validation, then the API, then the signed-in state.
```

The right step size is not the shortest or the longest one. It is the size that still lets you get a real signal inside 15 to 30 minutes.

<Callout title="Scenario: why coarse steps are the most dangerous" tone="warning">
Situation: AI generates the login page, registration page, API logic, and redirect behavior all at once. Action: do not celebrate too early. Ask yourself whether you could even identify which layer failed first. Why: the coarsest steps create the strongest illusion of progress while making diagnosis much harder.
</Callout>

## Step 2: A Good Step Must Satisfy Three Conditions
A strong implementation step usually satisfies all three:

| Condition | Why it matters | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Single scope | One step should solve one class of problem | Only build form validation |
| Short execution window | You should see progress within 15-30 minutes | Finish one pass and inspect it |
| Explicit completion signal | You should know what "done" means | An invalid email now shows a validation message |

If you cannot explain how to tell whether a step is complete, the step is usually still too fuzzy.

## Step 3: Put the Step Discipline into the AI Prompt Too
Stepwise implementation is not only for your notebook. It also needs to be visible to AI:

```text
Finish the current step and explain the verification method.
Do not move to the next step until I confirm.
```

That discipline line gives you the pacing control back. Otherwise AI will often continue into step two or step three before step one is even verified.

| Did you state the discipline? | What AI is more likely to do |
|---|---|
| No | Keep inventing future work on its own |
| Yes | Stop at the current step and wait for confirmation |

<Callout title="before / after: default forward motion vs forced pause" tone="info">
Wrong move: let AI continue automatically after finishing the current step. Better move: require it to stop, show the current result, and explain the verification method. The difference is whether risk gets pushed forward or resolved in the current round.
</Callout>

## Summary
- Coarse steps hide risk, and tiny steps drown you in management.
- A good step has a clear completion signal within a short working window.
- Step discipline only works if it is explicit to both you and the model.

## Methodology Review
The first discipline of closed-loop delivery is step design: make work small enough to verify, stop, and reverse.
