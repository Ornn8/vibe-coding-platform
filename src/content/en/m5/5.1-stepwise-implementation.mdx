<Callout title="Method Focus" tone="warning">
Closed-loop delivery starts when you stop asking for a full system in one shot and start designing work that can be verified step by step.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Why one prompt for everything usually fails

When you ask AI to build authentication, a database, an admin panel, and deployment in one go, it loses the chance to inspect the middle of the process. If one early assumption is wrong, everything that follows is built on a broken base.

The curriculum's point is not that AI is weak. The point is that large requests hide the moment where you should have stopped, checked, and corrected. One-shot delivery looks efficient until the first hidden error compounds into a full rewrite.

### Scenario

Compare two requests for the same app:
- Request A: "Build the whole app, including auth, CRUD, admin, and deployment."
- Request B: "First build login only, verify it, then add the database, then the protected page, then the admin surface."

The first request often returns a broad scaffold that seems complete but is difficult to trust. The second request gives you checkpoints you can inspect before the next step begins.

## Step 2: How to choose the right step size

A good step usually has three traits: one clear scope, progress that fits inside about 30 minutes, and a result you can verify right away. If the step is too big, risk gets hidden. If it is too small, you drown in management overhead.

This is why the curriculum contrasts three decompositions of the same feature: too coarse, too fine, and just right. A step is healthy when it can produce a visible signal of completion, not just "some progress."

### Scenario

For a user registration feature:
- Too coarse: "Implement registration" as one step
- Too fine: 20 tiny steps that each change one line
- Just right: 4-6 steps such as form UI, validation, API route, persistence, confirmation, and verification

The middle option is the only one that balances clarity with momentum.

## Step 3: Give AI a discipline rule

Stepwise work is not only for you. It also needs to be explicit to AI. A useful instruction is: finish the current step and verify it, and do not move to the next step until I confirm.

That single rule prevents AI from imagining future work on its own. It keeps you in control of pacing and makes verification the gate between steps. The curriculum wants you to practice this as a rule of collaboration, not as a preference.

### Scenario

Without the rule, AI often starts writing step 3 while step 1 is still unverified. With the rule, the assistant waits, asks for validation, and only then continues. That difference is what makes the workflow recoverable.

## Summary
- The goal of stepwise implementation is earlier feedback, not extra ceremony.
- A good step needs a clear completion signal.
- Keep AI on the current step until verification passes.

## Methodology Review
Closed-loop delivery starts when you move from impulse generation to step-based delivery.
