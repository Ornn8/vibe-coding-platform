## Step 1: Choose the Topic Before You Build

Pick a static site topic that is small but complete. A personal homepage, a reading list, a trip planner, an event invite, or a portfolio showcase all work well. What matters is having a clear theme and a manageable scope.

Before opening a tool, write down three things: who the site is for, what information matters most, and how many pages or sections you want. For a first project, three pages or fewer and five sections or fewer is a good rule.

## Step 2: Use the Plan to Drive Generation

Once you have a plan, writing the prompt becomes much easier. You are no longer asking for "a webpage"; you are describing a site for a specific audience with specific content and a clear style direction.

A useful prompt usually has three layers: goal, structure, and style. The goal tells the tool what kind of site this is. The structure tells it what the page should include. The style tells it what the visual direction should feel like.

For example:

```text
Build a three-page static site for a personal reading list.
Goal: help visitors understand what I am reading and why.
Structure: home page, current reads page, and contact page.
Style: warm, editorial, simple, and readable.
```

## Step 3: Iterate in the Right Order

The easiest mistake in a first project is worrying about details too early. Button radius, shadows, spacing, and image placement matter, but they should not steal attention from the main structure while the core is still unstable.

The safer order is: verify information structure first, then layout, then visual style, then polish. That gives every iteration a clear purpose and makes your learning easier to review later.

Common issues to avoid:

- Giving the tool too many unrelated requirements at once
- Describing the style in vague terms like "make it nice"
- Skipping page structure and jumping straight to colors
- Treating iteration as random trial and error instead of a sequence
